Making badass developers

What I liked the most about the Making Badass Developers video was the cognitive resource management. It's something I'll try to implement to upgrade my skills. Sierra says we have to divide our skills into three columns:

A. Can't do (but need to) --- B. Can do with effort --- C. Mastered

But from here we can run into some problems:

  • Problem 1 - Things don't get better (pile up on B)
  • Problem 2 - The intermediate blues (half assed skills in C)
  • Problem 3 - Takes too damn long (slow process)

To fix each one we can do the following:

  1. Keep more on A, Split B into subskills
  2. Revisit skills on C
  3. Bypass B where we can, speed up A and B

A crucial thing is pattern matching, our brain can have a quick notion of things being right or wrong if it has patterns to match. The key for this is to do high quality, very high quantity, examples.

How to talk to anyone

This week I was able to learn more about conversational skills. It's something that I definitely have a hard time on so this reading was very reflective. It inspired me and gave me ideas of areas I have to work on and tips to improve. The most important learnings that I got:

  • First impressions are important and there’s more to it than just what you talk about. Physical cues and actions are also important
  • Tips on how to start and continue great small talk. If you want to make the other person comfortable, or you want them to care about what you have to say, small talk is absolutely necessary.
  • To not to sound arrogant or a jerk, a conversation needs balance. You need to listen to what the other person has to say.
  • Getting inside a crowd can be done with a bit of research. Come prepared so you have something to talk about
  • Tips on how to make other people think you’re equals; this can open more doors for you and your proposals may be heard more if you have a connection with the other person you’re communicating with
  • How to modulate your voice or gestures. Quirks that will put people at ease when they’re communicating with you.
  • Treat every party, meeting, call as a rehearsal. You don’t know when the most important conversation of your life may happen.
  • Overlook the bloopers of whoever you’re communicating with So they don’t feel embarrassed.
  • How to ask for favors from other people so it feels like it’s done out of friendship. This may be the case sometimes, but well sometimes you know you want them to do you a favor for you at a later time.

What are the most profound life lessons from Stanford professor John Ousterhout?

I was able to reflect on why relationships fail and what we can do to prevent them from falling apart.

  • It’s really hard to build relationships that last for a long time
  • Relationships fail because of an accumulation of little things (professor Ousterhout calls them scar tissues) that build up over time and explode in a catastrophic event towards the end of the relationship.
  • Due to these scar tissues people start to become sensitive and they expect bad behavior. This will grow and grow until the tipping point where the relationship will fall apart.
  • If you want relationships to last a long time, whenever there is an issue, you have to solve it so none of the people involved will have lingering animosity or resentment.
  • The key is communication and compromise. Both sides need to understand the other’s point of view and be willing to compromise to achieve a solution That is fair for both of them.
  • Classic mistakes: being too nice. Whenever you have an issue, vocalize it.Also, trying to always win an argument is a sure way for scar tissue to grow and fracture your relationship.
